## **1. Situation Overview**

**Incident:**

* **Patient:** Teen athlete with **acute hand injury** sustained during sport.
* **Initial Point of Contact:** Remote care site.
* **Triage Path:** Remote site → HAP (Health Access Point) gatekeeper → SEH (Spoedeisende Eerste Hulp / Emergency Department).
* **Complicating Factor:** Father is both **hospital CEO and radiologist** at tertiary institution — potential influence on triage, transport, and care pathway decisions.

**Core Intel Gaps:**
A. Trauma capability level of *receiving facility* (SEH) unknown.
B. Specific capabilities and influence associated with *father’s institution* unclear.
C. Communication and escalation protocols between remote site and SEH not fully mapped.

---

## **2. Assessment**

### **A — Triage Integrity Under ‘Insider’ Pressure**

**Key Principles:**

* Triage decisions must be governed by **clinical urgency, objective criteria, and system protocols**, not provider status or institutional affiliation.
* **Adult hand trauma algorithms** prioritize vascular compromise, open fracture, compartment syndrome signs, neurovascular status, digit alignment, and timing for reduction/surgery.

**Risk Factors for Integrity Erosion:**

* *Perceived pressure* for diversion to father’s facility.
* *Unclear destination criteria* at remote site; absence of explicit trauma destination protocols.
* *Gatekeeper ambiguity* if HAP non-clinical or poorly empowered to enforce protocols.

**Assessment:**

* Current pathway shows **weaknesses** in ensuring destination decision is based purely on standardized triage criteria (e.g., using a regional trauma triage score).

---

### **B — Risk of Protocol Deviation**

**Threat Vectors:**

* Bypassing local trauma triage protocols to honor implicit requests.
* Directing patient transfer based on **institutional preference** rather than capability.
* **Overriding EMS/SEH decision support** without documented clinical justification.

**Consequences:**

* Increased morbidity from inappropriate destination choice.
* Delay to definitive care (e.g., revascularization window in complex hand fractures).
* Undermining of trauma system trust & compliance.

**Example Risk:**
If the child needs *microvascular repair* or advanced reconstructive surgery, transfer to a **non-trauma capable ED** (even if father’s) could delay needed intervention.

  * On-site orthopedic/hand surgery coverage,
  * Microsurgery capability,
  * Appropriate imaging modalities,
  * Anesthesia and vascular access services.

---

### **D — Long-Term Performance & Rehab Prognosis**

**Factors Influencing Prognosis:**

1. **Time to reduction/repair** — delays worsen long-term function.
2. **Neurovascular injury severity** — affects return to sport.
3. **Rehabilitation access** — structured hand therapy essential.
4. **Adolescent healing variability** — generally favorable but dependent on integrity of repair.

**Best-Case Functional Outcome:**

* Prompt, accurate reduction,
* Timely definitive surgery,
* Structured therapy → near-full return to sport (weeks–months).

**Worst-Case:**

* Missed compartment syndrome,
* Prolonged ischemia,
* Improper fixation → persistent disability, chronic pain, limited dexterity.

## **3. Systemic Vulnerabilities Identified**

**1) Destination Decision Weakness**

* Lack of rigor in applying formal triage destination rules *influence by status/power can override best practice*.

**2) Gatekeeper Authority Ambiguity**

* HAP unclear on authority to enforce protocol vs. deferring to advising parties.

**3) Information Cascading Failures**

* Unknown receiving capabilities put clinicians in blind transfer decisions.

**4) Conflict of Interest**

* Physician/CEO family member involvement introduces **latent bias** risk unless actively mitigated.

---

## **4. Recommendations for Protocols & Training**

### **Immediate**

* Standardize use of **trauma triage scorecards** (e.g., Canadian C-Spine/Harborview criteria adapted to extremity trauma).
* Mandate **receiving facility capability confirmation** before transfer acceptance.
* Empower HAP with **protocol enforcement authority**.

### **Short-Term**

* Implement **conflict of interest escalation policy**:

  * If family status presents possible deviation pressure → default to **protocol committee review**.
* Train EMS, SEH, and HAP teams on:

  * Objective triage criteria,
  * Avoiding “influence routing”,
  * Documentation requirements when exceptions occur.

### **Longitudinal**

* Establish **regional trauma network dashboards** showing capabilities & capacity in real time.
* Audit all trauma diversions quarterly with performance dashboards (time to care, outcomes).
